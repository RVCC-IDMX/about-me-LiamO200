    CSS Animation, A Brief History

    CSS Animation is a way for developers to animate elements of their HTML documents and spring them to life. When it was introduced in CSS3 in 2009 though, it wasn’t beloved by all. Many people believe CSS Animation lacks the flexibility and functionality of Js Animation which is powered by javascript. CSS Animation is able to modify the beginning and end states of an object, whilst Js Animation lets you get in between awarding the user more creative freedoms. This wasn’t it’s only controversy however, many believe it’s existence at all was an elaborate by apple to sidestep adobe flash. Flash was the predominant method of animation for the web for the longest time, but Apple wouldn’t let it function on IOS. IOS is one of the main ways people access the internet now, and it’s rising tide spelled out sure destruction for Adobe seeing as Steve Jobs himself slandered the company and their product by saying flash saps battery power, and it’s the #1 reason why mac products crash. They framed flash as being a “relic of the past”, and openly welcomed CSS Animation to function on safari. Adobe retorted refuting their claims saying it was a “smokescreen”. Jobs acknowledged their strained and drifting relationship saying that although adobe claims their rejection of flash was because of their restrictive and exclusionary business tactics, it’s the opposite and they’re guilty of exactly what they’re accusing. Being beholden to the whims of another third party company is indeed a risky endeavor. CSS animation isn’t reliant on the licenses and random decisions of adobe, which many may cite as the reason why it could be perceived it prevailed. In 2020 flash was discontinued, leaving a great sea of the internet a barren wasteland of errors. The reality is, these complicated stories of endless development wars seem to have no end. Before Js Animation there was Synchronized Multimedia Integration Language or SMIL which users utilized to animate elements. Every iteration in the world will be phased out by its successor whether we like it or not, and with the current trajectory of the internet the only power we have is to hope and pray whatever is next can be translated and archived properly to function on whatever is next. With the plethora of services flash provided, websites utilizing it either had to uproot their entire frameworks with programs to adapt their existing code, or scrap what they had entirely. This is something CSS Animation is less vulnerable to, but will inevitably one day fall victim to. Once the world is hooked up through eyeball projector monitor holograms sending data through neural link brain chips, CSS Animation will be a whisper in the wind spoken only by those who remember a day where little images squished and squashed happily along webpages, a simpler age. Here’s an example of CSS Animation right now! I’ve placed a little cat here for you to enjoy, before the netlify servers pitter out into the ethos with it’s bits and bytes dissipating and drifting through cyberspace, holding fractured memories of what once were 1s and 0s that played the very animation you were looking at now. Past then when we are both long gone, only nomad vagabonds who traverse a barren wasteland will access my hard drive with the smallest bits of recoverable memory will be able to piece it together past the thousands of flash animations i’ve downloaded and wrote it over with. Many may believe this endless crawl of time, every iteration replacing another is a sad nihilistic view of the world. But travel to the quietest mountaintops, and Buddhist monks will enlighten you that the law of impermanence is a beautiful thing. When the inevitable heat death of the universe envelops the final atom of the last brain in a jar containing the final computer programmer to ever remember CSS animation, it will finally be at peace. This little bouncing cat will reincarnate into something beyond our mortal comprehension, and the circle of its very life conceptually will travel again.
